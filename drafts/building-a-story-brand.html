Story Framework

  1. A character
  2. Has a problem
  3. and Meets a Guide
  4. Who gives them a plan
  5. and Calls Them to Action
  6. That ends in Success
  7. That helps them avoid Failure.

Tests

You should always be able to answer 3 questions, at any point:

  1. What does the hero want?
  2. Who or what is opposing the hero getting what they want?
  3. What will their life be like if the get (or don’t get) what they want?

The grunt test

Could a caveman look at your website and answer these questions:

  1. What do you offer?
  2. How will it make my life better?
  3. What do I need to do to buy it? (What’s next?)

It’s got to be really clear.

My notes on “Building A Story Brand” by Donald Miller

  1. Good marketing makes the benefit obvious. How does your product improve someones ability to “survive and thrive”?
  2. Don’t make the message confusing, there is a lot of noise in the world already. Cut through the noise by having a super quick and easy to understand message. Don’t be clever, be clear. Don’t make the customer spend calories trying to figure out how they will benefit from your product.
  3. Your marketing should follow the form of a story about survival. Everyone understands and enjoys stories, we gravitate towards them because it helps us make sense of the world and our lives.
  4. We are each the main character in our own lives, we are the hero in the story, with some problems and challenges than need solving.
  5. Stories are a great way to combat noise and get the attention of potential customers. This is because they organise information in an intuitive way and people are compelled to pay attention until all the “story gaps” have been closed.
  6. “Branding” is creating simple relevant messages that can be repeated over and over until they are “branded” into the public conscious.

Example:

When Apple released the Lisa computer in 1983, Jobs bought a 9 page ad in the New York times listing the computers features. When jobs returned to Apple after being fired, (and after partly founding Pixar, which tells stories) apple became a customer centric company, and their marketing was about the customers. 9 pages became 2 words - “think different”. A message about their customers, and their customers need to survive and thrive.

Apple isn’t the hero in the “think different” brand. The customer is. It’s the same with Nike. Apple plays a role more like Q in James Bond, giving the hero what they need to win.

About Stories

In its purest form a story starts out with a character living in peace and stability. Suddenly the stability is disrupted. Then the hero begins a journey to restore peace and stability that they used to enjoy.

The Hero

This is the customer, not you. You are the guide. The customer is the main character in their life. Everyone is the protagonist in their own life.

A story starts with a character who wants something. The first big question is “Will they get what they want?”. You need to define the characters ambition in the opening section, so that the listener knows what is at stake and what kind of a story it is.

What the customer wants is a great thing to design your business around

Story Gaps

Place a gap between a character and what they want. Will they find their way to success, overcome their challenges, etc.

A stories cadence and momentum is defined by the ebb and flow of story gaps opening and closing.

If you fail to define something that the customer wants, you fail to open a story gap, and this makes the story uninteresting. There is no question that demands an answer.

Story gaps work because we want things to resolve. It’s like singing twinkle twinkle little star but stopping before the “are” on the last line. You need to hear the last note of the melody for the tune to feel complete.

Pare down the customers desire to a single focus. Make your brand known for single specific desire and helping people get it.

Don’t clutter the story by diluting the hero’s desire with other desires. You can eventually develop subplots.

Survival

Choose a desire relevant to their survival.

  • conserving resources
    • financial
    • time
  • building a social network
  • gaining status
  • accumulating resources
  • being generous
    • most people are not as Darwinian as we’ve been led to believe
    • people are empathetic and caring,
  • the desire for meaning.
    • invite them to participate in something greater than themselves
    • “the chief desire of man is not pleasure, but meaning.”
    • give customers an opportunity to be generous.

Translate your message into several survival categories - put your solution in the context of something (a type of survival) they they want. Otherwise there is no story gap.

Branding

Customers want to know where you can take them. Unless you can take them somewhere they want to go, why would they listen?

The marketing goal is that every potential customer knows where we want to take them. Define a desire for your customer, and your marketing story will have a powerful hook.

Problem

There are three types of problem

  1. Internal
  2. External
  3. Philosophical

Customers are much more motivated to solve internal problems than they are external.

Guide

All heros need a guide - if they didn’t need one they wouldn’t have a problem and their wouldn’t be a story in the first place. Most (all) people are looking for a guide to help them solve their problems and win their day.

Plan

Heros trust a guide who has a plan.

People are looking for a philosophy then can embody, or a series of steps they can take to solve their problems.

Two kinds of plan

  • Agreement Plan
  • Process Plan

Both types work by earning trust and offer the custom a clear path to stability.

A Call To Action

Heros don’t take action by themselves, they must be challenged. It’s just not interesting if the hero can solve their own problems without an externality, this is just how stories (and humans) work.

Characters only take action when challenged by an outside force.

A call to action is a clear and direct step a customer can take to overcome their problem and return to a peaceful life.

Two kinds of Call-To-Action

  • Direct (purchase something now, book an appointment, subscribe.)
  • Transitional (further the relationship)

Use both kinds in your messaging, and customers will understand (without burning calories) what you (the guide) want them to do (the plan) to solve their problem.

Then they can decide if they will let us play a role in their story.

What is at stake?

Stories live and die by a single question; “What is at stake?” If nothing can be gained or lost then nobody cares. If there is nothing at stake in the story then there is no story.

If there is no benefit to buying the product, then why buy it?

You have to show the customer the cost of not buying the product.

Success

Show the customer a vision of how great their life will be if they do business with you.

Lastly

Not all elements of a story should be used evenly.
